Public health systems need transparency and accountability to create impact

Across India, community health workers like Bharti spend 50 hours every month recording data across 10 registers and 5 mobile applications — feeding 50 portals that rarely talk to each other.

This data serves a reporting function, not a care function. High-risk mothers are identified but never followed up. Malnourished children are flagged in one department and lost in another. The system collects, but it doesn't close the loop.

Who we are

We believe, with unshakable conviction, in the power of data-driven public health systems to save lives at the last mile.

Photograph: The original NFC necklace on a child in Udaipur, circa 2015

Born in the field. Built for systems change.

Khushi Baby began in 2014 with a question posed in a Yale engineering classroom: how do you close the immunization gap for families at the last mile? The first answer was a small NFC necklace replacing the traditional thread mothers in Udaipur already trusted — carrying a digital health record.

A decade of field research, rigorous trials, and hard-won government trust later, we operate one of the largest platforms for community health workers in the world — now spanning all primary health care verticals from maternal health to tuberculosis, immunization to nutrition, and climate adaptation.

Our next phase focuses on completing care coordination loops — closing the gap between identification and treatment across every health program — and sharing what works as open playbooks for the public health ecosystem.

Photograph: CHIP dashboard review with state officials

Data driving care to the most vulnerable

Khushi Baby strengthens public health systems by closing the gap between data, insights, and action — particularly at the last mile of service delivery.

Converting data to action for public health departments

Khushi Baby operates as a technical partner to ministries of health — working in a build-operate-transfer model that scales through existing government systems rather than building new ones alongside them.

This keeps operating costs low, builds local ownership at every level of government, and produces a model that the state can adopt, fund, and run independently.

Our growth follows government demand, unmet need, and invitation from partners already working in a geography. Where we operate, we aim to go deeper before we go wider.

Start from the ground

Understand the felt need before reaching for the technology

Every deployment begins by sitting with health leadership to identify the most pressing priority — whether maternal mortality, child malnutrition, TB, or immunisation gaps. We establish a baseline, identify where the care cascade breaks, and define what closing the loop looks like here.

This is not a rapid assessment. It takes field visits and co-design with community health workers — who understand barriers to care that no dashboard will ever surface on its own.

A solution designed for a problem we don't fully understand will be used once, then abandoned. We have spent 250,000 hours in the field to earn the right to build something that lasts.

Photograph: CHW using CHIP during household census in a tribal village

The data foundation

Conduct a digital health census at the village level

Community health workers use CHIP to enumerate every household — establishing a live registry of named, located individuals that every health program can track over time. This is the substrate on which all insights and actions depend. Without it, targeting is directional at best.

The census maps multi-dimensional vulnerability — social, economic, geographic, and health — and integrates with national platforms so data collected once flows across programs.

Decision-grade insights

Surface what matters at every level of the system

Raw data becomes actionable through AI and GIS dashboards that show where high-risk populations are concentrated, where referral loops are breaking, and which individuals need follow-up before their window closes.

Insights flow to officials at block, district, and state level — including chief minister offices — and are directly linked to action scheduling.

A district official who can see — at 9am — exactly which 40 mothers need a follow-up call today, and assign it before the day begins, is working inside a fundamentally different kind of public health system.

Health Action Centers

Activate an embedded action unit where need is deepest

In aspirational districts, Khushi Baby co-establishes a Health Action Center with the health department. A lean interdisciplinary unit coordinates care escalations, referral follow-ups, and daily outreach that would otherwise fall through the cracks — tracked, acted on, and measured.

The HAC does not replace government health workers. It coordinates them.

The DIA Loop in Practice

Nandurbar Health Action Center

A district-owned command center that turns routine health data into real-time action — ensuring no high-risk mother or child falls through the cracks.

"She had carried her pregnancy entirely alone — no checkup, no contact, no record in any system. She existed, but the system could not yet see her."

Nandurbar sits in northwestern Maharashtra — dense forests, hilly terrain, deeply rooted tribal communities. 69% Scheduled Tribe population. One of India's 112 Aspirational Districts. Maternal mortality 4× the state average.

🗺️

CHIP Dashboard: Nandurbar District

CHIP census surveys 87.7% of 1.8M residents. 47+ datasets integrated: WhatsApp group intelligence, SNCU discharge lists, health worker nominations, ad hoc surveys. 2,085 ASHAs digitally enabled.

A

Four call center operators follow up with 500+ highest-risk weekly. Field team coordinates home visits, transport, investigations. Weekly government reviews convert gaps into time-bound actions.

The loop closing — March 2026

HAC Associates identified 3 high-risk newborns in Akkalkuwa, Dhadgaon, and Taloda blocks who had not received mandated home-based newborn care visits. Through 1–2 targeted follow-ups and coordination with frontline workers, all three were converted to compliance. Two low-birth-weight children showed significant weight gain. In one case, the child's weight was recorded for the first time since birth.

This is what data → insight → action looks like for one family.

The CHIP Platform

Community Health Integrated Platform — unified digital infrastructure for last-mile public health delivery.

Screenshot: CHIP app showing household census flow

One platform. All programs.

CHIP consolidates the entire work requirement of frontline health workers across all primary health care programs into a single interface — reducing 800+ fragmented indicators across 12 national programs. Validated through a 3,200-patient RCT and deployed across 85,000 health workers reaching 60 million people.

Diagram: CHIP integration architecture

CHIP as an integration layer

Connected to 11 state and central government platforms — Ayushman Bharat, Poshan Tracker, Ni-kshay, and more. These integrations link a malnourished child identified in nutrition to their treatment in health. They allow TB surveillance to draw on a 40M-person census. Each integration unlocks new network effects.

Screenshot: CHIP dashboard in use by officials

CHIP as a measurement tool

Enables partners to measure what works by observing real-time village-level data. Used to evaluate the CHO cadre roll-out in Rajasthan, inform the Maharashtra Tribal Health Report 2025, and plan new emergency health facility distribution. Powers India's first village-level climate-health vulnerability maps.

Bridging the malnutrition treatment gap across departments

NURTURE links Poshan Tracker identification with treatment pathways across nutrition and health departments. ICDS supervisors and field monitors drive assessment completion and VCDC/NRC referral follow-through.

In Nandurbar: 680 children assessed in Q1 2026, 329 confirmed SAM, 207 started VCDC referral out of 324 referred. Field monitors now trained to convert non-compliant NRC transfers into completed admissions.

Key result: 72K SAM suspects assessed statewide. Dashboard owned by DWCD, reviewed by Chief Minister's Social Sector War Room.
Photograph: TB screening camp, Rajasthan

Targeted TB surveillance across 40 million people

CHIP census identified 10M vulnerable people for priority screening. Five surveillance rounds revealed that universal screening was missing cases. A vulnerability-targeted strategy — adopted by the national TB program — transformed detection efficiency.

In the latest cycle: 15,956 presumptive cases received follow-up tests, 1,265 diagnosed positive, and 1,217 initiated treatment — closing the loop from identification through to care.

Key result: 40K+ suspects in one cycle. 8× detection rate increase. Strategy adopted by Government of India. Nikshay integration now in field testing.

Finding the children the system missed

Statewide census of children under two. Geospatial hotspot mapping created district action plans. Coordinated field follow-up closed the gap.

Key result: 96K+ zero-dose identified, 6,700+ immunised.
Screenshot: CHVI climate-health vulnerability map

India's first village-level climate-health vulnerability index

CHVI combines CHIP social vulnerability data with Google Earth Engine and Indian Meteorological Department layers. Zonal workshops across all 7 zones of Rajasthan used CHVI outputs to identify high-vulnerability blocks and priority populations. Districts mapped specific interventions — adjusted working hours, onsite medical camps, ORS distribution, facility preparedness — across health, labour, education, and rural development departments.

Key result: 15+ district action plans informed. Multisectoral response now operational across Rajasthan for the 2026 heat season.
Photograph: Family planning counselling in Pali district

Closing the loop for unmet family planning need

CHIP and NFHS data identified Pali district — lowest modern contraceptive use, highest unmet need in Rajasthan. 5 priority sectors selected. Sector-level analysis of eligible couples pinpointed where to intervene. The state issued a formal mandate letter directing health workers to use CHIP due-lists for targeted outreach.

ASHAs have visited 413 households with unmet need — 268 have agreed to adopt an appropriate family planning method. Early data shows a 38.7% increase in Antara uptake in intervention areas.

Key result: 310K+ beneficiaries screened statewide. Live DIA loop closure with measurable service delivery changes underway in Pali.
